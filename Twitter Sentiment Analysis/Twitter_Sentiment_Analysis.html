Trading on Twitter engagement — a sentiment-ranked equity strategy

Ranks stocks monthly by their Twitter engagement ratio (comments ÷ likes), goes long the top 5 highest-engagement names, rebalances at each month-end, and benchmarks cumulative returns against QQQ. A clean test of whether retail social activity predicts near-term price performance.

The signal construction respects look-ahead bias: sentiment ranks are computed on month-end data, and the portfolio is shifted forward by one day (pd.DateOffset(1)) so that the position is only entered at the start of the following month — simulating realistic execution timing.

Signal construction
Engagement ratio as alpha signal
Engagement ratio
comments ÷ likes
Higher ratio = more discussion relative to passive approval. Debate-heavy stocks tend to have higher near-term volatility and momentum.
Activity filter
likes > 20 AND comments > 10
Removes low-signal noise. Stocks with minimal Twitter presence get filtered before ranking to avoid rank instability from tiny denominators.
Monthly aggregation
mean(engagement_ratio)
Daily ratios averaged to monthly frequency to smooth out single-day spikes from earnings announcements or news events.
Cross-sectional rank
rank(ascending=False)
Each month, all stocks ranked against each other. Only rank 1–5 are held. This removes the level effect — the absolute ratio doesn't matter, only relative position.
Why engagement ratio over raw sentiment score? Raw sentiment (positive/negative) is widely used and likely arbitraged. The comments-to-likes ratio is a contrarian proxy — high debate relative to endorsement often signals market disagreement, which can precede price moves.

Methodology
Design decisions
1
No look-ahead bias
Sentiment ranks computed on month-end, portfolio entered at month-start + 1 day via DateOffset(1). Rank information is never used on the same day it's available.
2
Engagement over raw sentiment
Comments ÷ likes captures the ratio of active discussion to passive approval — a less crowded signal than NLP-based positive/negative scoring which is widely used and potentially arbitraged.
3
Cross-sectional ranking
Stocks ranked against each other each month rather than against their own history. Removes time-series drift in engagement levels — only relative engagement matters for selection.
4
Log returns for compounding
np.log(Adj Close).diff() for returns, np.exp(np.log1p(r).cumsum()) - 1 for cumulative. Log returns are additive across time, preventing compounding distortions in the backtest.
5
QQQ as benchmark
NASDAQ-100 ETF chosen because Twitter engagement data skews toward high-growth tech and consumer names — the same universe QQQ captures. S&P 500 would create a sector mismatch.
Honest limitations
What this doesn't account for
!
Transaction costs
Monthly rebalancing across 5 stocks incurs real bid-ask spread and commission costs. A realistic implementation would need to net these against gross returns, especially for smaller positions.
!
Survivorship bias
Stocks that were delisted or went bankrupt during 2021–2023 may not appear in the dataset. This could overstate returns if the worst performers are excluded.
!
Short backtest window
2 years (2021–2023) covers one bull and one bear regime. The strategy hasn't been tested across a full market cycle — results may not generalise.
!
Concentration risk
Holding only 5 equal-weighted names means idiosyncratic events in a single stock have outsized impact. A production version would need position sizing and risk limits.
